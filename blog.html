10+1 watercolor tools you will need as a beginner

Find Out Which Tools Are Already Available At Your Home

Finding the right tools as a watercolor beginner can be overwhelming and confusing – especially when you don’t have a guide as to what you actually need to get started.

But I’ll tell you one thing for sure, fancy tools won’t make anyone a better watercolorist, practice does. Read my list about the essential watercolor tools and save a fortune. And the best part of this list – most of the tools are already available at your home.

Read more...

10. Mixing pallette

A mixing pallette is needed for color mixing as they allow you blend and test colors easily. And an old plastic plate will do the work perfectly! I mix my colors using this tip to produce some of the paintings you see.

9. Jar of water

This one’s a no-brainer. I mean how else do you plan to mix watercolor paints with no water? Try not to complicate things though. Two jars you won’t use anymore would do the work. 0.3 – 0.5 liters jars are enough.

8. Hairdrier

This watercolor technique can save you so much time. Use a hairdrier to speed up the drying of a layer. That way you can paint the next layer without messing up your painting.

7. Spray bottle

You can use an empty spray bottle for helping you creating loose watercolor backgrounds.

6. Paper towel

A paper towel should always be within reach, so if you accidentally spill some paint/water you can quickly mop up the mistake from your paper. It also helps in drying your brushes in between use.

5. Masking tape

You’ll need a tape to secure your paper to a board so it won’t buckle as you paint. I use regular masking tape you can buy at any hardware store, however I saw other artists using washi tapes for this reason, so basically use whatever you have.

4. Used toothbrush

Perfect for splattering techniques, I especially love using toothbrushes for creating starry effects on galaxy skies.

3. Watercolor paper

Do not make the mistake of trying to save money by using a cardboard paper, or a copy paper as this could cost you a lot. Use watercolor paper for watercolor painting. What’s a good quality watercolor paper?

  • It’s acid free

  • Weighs at least 150 gramms – less than 150 gramms wc papers tend to buckle and warp

  • Hot press paper: And if you are a beginner I’d recommend hot press over cold press, since hot press paper is not as rough surfaced as cold press wc paper – so personally I think you will have an easier time painting on hot press paper.

  • On the other hand, cold press papers have a rougher surface which creates beautiful texture on the painting. You can try both to decide your preferred paper.


2. Watercolor brushes

You don’t need to buy brushes with natural hair because that’s expensive, but neither should you use cheap brushes which are not made for watercolor painting. Use instead synthetic watercolor brushes which can mimic the properties of natural hair brushes.

Also, you don’t need a lot of brushes, 3-4 brushes are enough to start. A small, medium and a big one.

  • Small: size 3
  • Medium: size 6-7
  • Big: size 12


1. Watercolor paints - Basic color palette

Watercolor paints come in different forms including tubes, pan and liquid watercolor. Talking about quality there’s also kid’s watercolor paints, student grade paints, and artist’s grade paints.

You don’t need to spend a lot of money when you start learning watercolors, however, it’s important to use paints with rich pigmention. So, I suggest that you start with 6 colors of artist grade paints, so you don’t waste your money on student quality watercolors.
